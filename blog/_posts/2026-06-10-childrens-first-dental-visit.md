## When Should a Child First Visit the Dentist?

The ideal time is **by their first birthday** or within 6 months of the first tooth appearing. Surprised? Most parents wait until there's pain — by then, decay is already advanced.

Early visits help us catch problems early, guide proper habits, and most importantly — help your child feel **comfortable and unafraid** at the dental clinic.

## Why Baby Teeth Matter (Even Though They Fall Out)

Many parents believe milk teeth don't need treatment since they'll fall out anyway. This is a costly myth:

- Baby teeth **hold space** for permanent teeth — early loss causes crooked adult teeth
- Decayed baby teeth cause **pain and infection** that can damage the permanent tooth developing underneath
- Children with dental pain **eat poorly and lose focus** in school
- Healthy baby teeth are essential for **clear speech development**

## Common Dental Problems in Children

**Early childhood caries (bottle decay)** — caused by milk or juice at bedtime. Never put a child to sleep with a milk bottle.

**Cavities** — the most common chronic childhood disease, more common than asthma.

**Thumb sucking** — normal until age 3–4; beyond that, it can push teeth forward.

**Dental injuries** — chipped or knocked-out teeth from falls and sports.

## How to Prepare Your Child for the Dentist

- Use positive words — say "the doctor will count your teeth" instead of mentioning pain, injections, or drilling
- **Never use the dentist as a threat** ("If you eat chocolate, the doctor will pull your tooth!")
- Read picture books about dental visits
- Visit in the morning when children are fresh and cooperative
- Stay calm yourself — children sense parental anxiety

## Preventive Treatments for Children

**Fluoride application** — strengthens enamel and prevents cavities. Quick and painless.

**Pit and fissure sealants** — protective coating on the grooves of back teeth where most cavities start. One of the most effective preventive treatments available.

## Daily Care Tips by Age

- **0–1 year:** wipe gums with a clean cloth after feeding
- **1–3 years:** brush with a rice-grain smear of fluoride toothpaste
- **3–6 years:** pea-sized toothpaste, parent assists brushing
- **6+ years:** child brushes, parent supervises until age 8–9
